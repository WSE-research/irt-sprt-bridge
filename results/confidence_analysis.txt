Why Confidence Ordering Fails for SPRT Certification
=======================================================

Finding: Confidence ordering is 4.4% WORSE than random for SPRT test length.
Difficulty ordering (cross-model agreement) saves 41.3% (LOO-CV).

Root cause: LLM confidence is a weak, dataset-dependent signal.

Per-dataset confidence behavior:
  ASAP-SAS: conf positively correlates with difficulty (r=+0.23)
            -> models are MORE confident on EASIER items
            -> low-confidence-first = slightly harder items first
            -> but signal too weak to help (conf ordering +7.5% worse than random)

  SciEntsBank: conf NEGATIVELY correlates with difficulty (r=-0.21)
               -> models are MORE confident on HARDER items (anti-informative)
               -> low-confidence-first = EASIER items first (exactly backwards)
               -> this is the anti-informative confidence from NeurIPS Section 5

  Mohler: weak positive correlation (r=+0.17), nearly neutral effect

Key insight: Cross-model difficulty is a POPULATION-level signal (stable across
models). Confidence is a MODEL-level signal (varies by model AND dataset in
unpredictable ways, can be anti-informative). Population-level signals are more
reliable for certification ordering.

Confidence gap (correct vs wrong): only 0.10 globally.
Models are barely more confident when correct than when wrong.
This is consistent with H10 (confidence-faithfulness dissociation).
